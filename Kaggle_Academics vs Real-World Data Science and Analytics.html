Kaggle/Academics vs Real-World Data Science and Analytics

0 Comments · 83 Views
Kashyap Bhuva 07 Apr 2020

Targeted to the beginners in Data Science who are seeking a first job in Data Science Analytics

Kaggle/Academics vs Real-World Data Science and Analytics

 

“I switched my career to data science getting lured by those reports of Harvard Business Review, IBM etc. I have finished an entire online specialization on data science. I am strong at Python. I have built machine learning models like …….… in my Kaggle projects. I am really good at building ML models. Despite many opportunities available for data science, I am still finding it very difficult to land a first job. Why are recruiters much more interested in hiring data scientists having relevant internships/work experience? “

 If these are the questions you are asking to yourself, you came to the right place today. The real-world data science is drastically different than what the technical courses and academic projects could teach. That’s the sole reason, why recruiters want you to have past data science internships/experience on your profile before hiring. Having a strong technical background in data science always helps, but real-world experience adds a cherry on the cake.

In this article, we would understand this difference between an academic project and real-world project using a very common analytics problem of churn (customer retention) modelling. By the end of the article, all the questions you had (paragraph 1) would be answered. If you are a good story-teller, you may be able to project an academic project on your resume as a real-world industry-sponsored project, and appear to be a more promising candidate in your next interview.

                                                                       Fig 1

 

The Analytics Process: (For a Churn Prediction Data Science Project)

1) Defining the Problem:

Kaggle:

Read the problem statement for customer churn. Explore the columns to get an understanding of data. Jump into the data.

Real-World:

Problem Statement from the management is very vague. We must finalize upon what’s the exact problem we are dealing with. The problem statement couldn’t change in the later course of the project.

Firstly, we need to define Churn. Churn could be a permanent churn or a temporary churn (wherein customer returns after a certain time) depending on the domain. Netflix, streaming service providers have temporary churn phenomenon. we need to define a threshold no. of de-activation days to call that to be a churn. Say eg. D+15, D+30 (D+X=No. of days after De-Activation). This definition requires a lot of brainstorming, domain knowledge and finally management’s approval. In a temporary churn problem, it may so happen that a subscriber churns multiple time. Whereas in permanent churn problems, the customer never returns. Both temporary and permanent churn has a different approach. The problem statement for any churn project would be to identify the high-risk churn customers and try to retain them.

 2) Checking the Feasibility. Eg. Availability of Data and other Resources Required:

Kaggle:

It must be feasible enough, to be uploaded on this platform. All the datasets are available. There’s no external data required, unavailability of which might have rendered the problem non-feasible.

Real-World:

Is solving this problem feasible? Do we have the availability of the required data and resources? Is it possible to implement?

Can we think of a few factors including customer demographics, customer-product characteristics, external variables that may be leading to churn? Can the data for those factors be availed?

If the model is ready, is it possible to implement? May not be always. What is the cost involved in the implementation? Eg. For a banking domain problem of loan churn (balance transfer), even if we got the high-risk customers using Machine Learning, since all the loan terms are fixed, there is nothing you can offer your customer to retain him/her. Even if the rate of interest is variable and customers are contacted to inform that they are eligible for a lower rate of interest, all false positives will lead to a huge loss to the bank. So, there may be no point of doing churn modelling in such cases.

 3) Estimating the ROI, Expected Gains:

Kaggle:

No need. Not asked.

Real-World:

Is solving this problem worth it?

For a predictive modelling project like Churn, associating approximate gain per each of the True Positives, True Negatives and loss per each of the False Positives and False Negatives, we could estimate the ROI. It is all-inclusive of the cost of execution of the project, implementation of the project, etc. If the ROI is less, there is no point in doing this project.

4) Classifying the Problem: Descriptive/Predictive/Prescriptive: Predictive

Both Kaggle and Real-World:

Since we are predicting the probability of churn, this is a predictive analytics project. If the problem was to identify which product has the highest churn, then it is a descriptive analytics project, and if the problem is to find the optimal selling price at which the churn could be least, that becomes a prescriptive analytics project. To be able to select the necessary tools and resources, it is important to classify a problem in a suitable bin.

4.2) Predictive:

Let’s directly talk about predictive analytics, since we are dealing with churn modelling.

4.2a) Check again, whether simple profiling could fetch you the high-risk population instead?

Kaggle:

That’s not what we are asked to submit.

Real-World:    

Doing a bivariate visualization of each variable with the target variable churn, we could know the impact of each variable on Churn. Using a few important variables, we could get high-risk profiles/characteristics and find their lift using simple SQL queries. For eg. Customers with age 30-35, location New York City, vintage of 3 years are 2.6 times more likely to churn. Finding a few of those high-risk profiles and only targeting them, could save us from investing time and resources behind Machine Learning. We need to compare the ROI for both methods before jumping into Machine Learning.

4.2b) Decide the Unit of Analysis, Target Variable and  Rows to be included

Kaggle:

The primary key of each row is the unit of analysis. We want to find the prediction for each row. Target variable column is already available. Thus, both the unit of analysis and the target variable are all set to be included in the model base.

Real-World:

Let’s discuss temporary churn problem in a streaming service company (like Netflix). We must ask a question that what are we predicting exactly? This is not as simple as it seems. Are we just predicting the probability of a customer to churn? Surprisingly No. Probability changes with time. Probability of a Customer A to churn in Jan 2020 could be different than Oct 2019. We are predicting the probability that a customer will churn at a particular time. We want to find a high-risk profile/characteristic leading to churn and not a high-risk customer leading to churn. Hence, a row in the model base would correspond to a customer profile at an instance of time. If we consider time in months, the model base will have ‘n’ number of entries/rows for the same customer, where ‘n’ is the Vintage (Age on Network in Months) of the customer. Thus,

Unit of Analysis= {Customer, Time (Month)}

The Target Variable definition should be exactly the same as it is defined in the Problem Statement from part 1). Assume we defined: De-activation+15 days as Target Variable (Churn)

In this case, the row in the model base shouldn’t just represent the latest customer data, but customer data, with respect to time (month here).

                                Table 1

Let’s take an example. I joined a streaming subscription in Jan 2018. Recharge date is 1st of every month (assume its not an auto-recharge). If I don’t recharge, I am De-activated. If I don’t recharge till 15th of that month, I would be considered as churned in that month. Thus, the target variable can also be defined as de-activate till 15th of a month.

Say, I churned in Jan 2019, returned in Mar 2019, again churned in May 2019, returned in Aug 2019. What is the probability I will churn in April 2020 if I deactivate (Don’t recharge on April 1st)? 

Table 2: My data as on 2nd of every Month and Target variable as on the last day of a Month

This is how the rows of my data would look like in a model base. It consists of my data on a monthly level. Likewise, there would be historical data for all the customers. Thus, it is for a customer at a particular time that we are predicting the probability of churn.

Selecting only the relevant observations:

We should think a bit about the model deployment before selecting the observations to be included in the model base. Say for eg. The company is planning to call high-risk customers and offer them discounts to retain them. Would they want to call the active customers too? Doesn’t make sense. They would only want to call the customers who didn’t recharge on the 1st of the month and are de-activated currently to prevent them from D+15 (Churn). Having said that, we only want to find the probability of a de-activated customer to churn and leave aside the active ones. This leaves us excluding the customer profiles of those months in which recharge was done (the customer was active all-time) from the model base consider only the de-activation instances. (This was identified using “Recharged on 1st itself” variable)

c) Do feature engineering manually, create new features, modify existing features

Kaggle:

Other than feature encoding, there is less scope of creating new features from the existing, modify existing features.

Real-World:

Real World problem completely relies on feature engineering. There’s no spoon-feeding of the features. We must get data from multiple sources, transform it into usable form and create new features out of it to be included in the model base. This requires extensive use of data manipulation, joins. etc. SQL, NoSQL would be better for this task than Python/R. In the churn prediction problem for the streaming service, we could create variables like:

We must be creative here. This is more of an art than science. We must know the domain in and out to be able to create new variables.

We could also combine multiple variables to form a new variable. Or modify an existing variable to increase its predictive power.

 d) Exploratory Data Analysis

Kaggle:

A few graphs to understand the data on Python/R. Check the distribution. Etc. No points for presentation, focus on getting models more fine-tuned.

Real-World:

The management wants a dashboard with slicers, showing all the variable relationships. They won’t be convinced with how much ever visualizations you show them. Many Adhoc reports would be requested every time. Communicating the results is an essential part of the work and it couldn’t be possible without adequate exploratory data analysis and data digging. Generally, tools like Excel, Tableau, PowerBI etc. are used in the industry for EDA.

 e) Get rid of multicollinearity, do feature selection:

Both Kaggle and Real-World:

Equal work involved in both.

 f) Try different ML/DL Algorithms, Evaluate the models on validation and test data:

Kaggle:

Try many algorithms. Do hyper-parameter tuning. Use the most complex algorithms. Switch to DL. All you must do is minimize the error. Focus on Accuracy!! Rise above the scoreboard.

Real-World:

Accuracy is important, but money is more important. Try to maximize the Return on Investment (Coming on it later). Build a model which would drive more profits. Management won’t understand and approve black-box approaches. Use simple interpretable algorithms like Logistic Regression, Classification, Trees, Regularization etc. Avoid Deep Learning.

ghij) Model Deployment and Implementation, Automate, Track and Refurbish:

Kaggle:

Project is over. Just upload the results. Focus on the scoreboard.

Real-World:

There is no use of prediction if the models are not implemented correctly. It takes a lot of brainstorming and domain expertise for making a model deployment strategy. How much of population to contact? How to contact? Whom to contact? When to contact? Are the expected gains more than the cost of implementation?

Let’s take an example of a churn model for bank loans. Machine Learning gives us the probability of each customer to churn (Balance Transfer) next month.

 

How much of population to contact?

How many high-risk customers to target? We need to set up a probability threshold at which the gains are maximum.

                                                         Fig 2

To set a probability threshold, we must consider the costs associated with TP, FP, TN, FN stop at the probability at which the Return is Maximum.

Return= Expected Profit due to Each TP and TN – Expected Loss due to each FP and FN

How to contact?

Means of communication. What to offer for retaining them? Lower interest rate, or any other benefit.

Whom to contact?

Are we only considering the churn probability to determine the population to target? We must include the financial loss involved in that churn too. A customer having a low probability of churn but a heavy loss if he churns, he must be contacted.

When to contact?

Few days before the EMI payment date? Immediately after? Or anytime.

Are the expected gains more than the cost of implementation?

If the high-risk customers are offered a lower interest rate to retain them, false positives would cause a huge loss to the banks. Are we still left with enough profit? If not, everything is in vain.

 

After all of this is accomplished, automate the model to score the new data whenever required. Usually scoring of the customers is done every month. After the month is over, we need to track the model performance and calculate how much did we save for the company. The implementation part may seem more challenging than the predictive modelling part. If the performance of the model starts dropping gradually every month, it could be likely because the relationships between independent variables and dependent variables are changing with respect to time. The model requires refurbishment. In highly dynamic situations, models would frequently demand refurbishment.

Hope you got a feel of how complex things are in the real world.

Note: This article is not intended to be critical of the academic courses or the Kaggle platform.
